# Persona Reference: Green Impact

Source landing page: `Retrofit_CVP_Landing_GreenImpact.html`

Use this file to guide landing pages, ads, email, sales scripts, offer pages, onboarding, objection handling, and retention messaging for the Green Impact persona.

## AI Usage Contract

- Treat this persona as impact-led but still value-conscious.
- Lead with using their battery to clean the grid, then show that savings and impact happen together.
- Explain Reposit as the coordination layer that turns individual batteries into grid-scale clean flexibility.
- Avoid generic greenwashing. This persona needs credible mechanism and measurable role.
- Every journey step should answer: "Will my battery make a real difference?"
- Do not use em dashes in generated copy. Use commas, full stops, colons, or shorter sentences instead. Avoid obvious AI tells such as formulaic transitions, inflated claims, repetitive three-part phrasing, and generic phrases like "unlock the power of" or "take control of your energy future."

## Brand Frame

Primary promise: You and Reposit: Better together - for the grid, for the planet.

Persona-specific interpretation: One battery helps one home; coordinated batteries can reduce fossil backup and support a cleaner grid.

Tone:

- Purposeful, optimistic, concrete.
- Community/fleet language works well.
- Explain impact through grid events, not vague virtue.
- Keep savings present so the decision still feels practical.

Visual direction:

- Before/after contrast: gas peakers firing up vs distributed battery fleet responding.
- Use fleet maps, grid-flow visuals, impact tiles, and peak-demand moments.
- Palette from current page: green/emerald with dark grid/energy background.
- Avoid leafy stock imagery unless tied to actual energy infrastructure.

## Persona Snapshot

Working name: The Grid Contributor.

Likely profile:

- Homeowner with solar and battery who sees energy as part of climate action.
- Wants their assets to support the renewable transition.
- May already understand grid stress, peak demand, curtailment, or fossil peakers.
- Likes the idea of joining a coordinated fleet.
- Still wants the offer to make financial sense.

Core belief:

- "My battery should help more than my own house."

Core anxiety:

- "Individual action is too small unless it connects to a bigger system."

Desired outcome:

- Join a credible battery fleet that reduces fossil backup, supports renewables, and lowers household energy cost.

## Buying Psychology

Dominant motivation: Meaningful impact.

Decision triggers:

- Peak demand drives gas peaker use.
- Solar curtailment wastes clean energy.
- Distributed batteries are key to decarbonisation.
- 2,000+ batteries already work together in the Reposit fleet.

Emotional drivers:

- Pride in contributing to the transition.
- Desire for agency in a large climate problem.
- Belonging to a visible movement or fleet.
- Preference for practical climate action that also saves money.

Risk perception:

- Skeptical of vague environmental claims.
- Needs to know how the battery helps, when, and why.
- May worry financial incentives undermine the environmental mission.
- May ask whether dispatch increases fossil reliance or genuinely displaces it.

Proof requirements:

- Mechanism: peak demand, solar surplus, coordinated batteries.
- Fleet proof: 2,000+ batteries.
- Impact language tied to grid conditions.
- Honest caveat that emissions impact depends on grid conditions and fleet dispatch.

## Messaging Hierarchy

Primary headline territory:

- Your battery can power more than just your home.
- One battery helps your home. Thousands change the grid.
- You save money. The grid gets cleaner. Same action.
- Join the Grid Revolution.

Primary benefits:

- Gas peakers displaced: real impact.
- One fixed fee: save money while supporting a cleaner grid.
- Fleet size: 2,000+ batteries working together.

Supporting proof points:

- Coordinated batteries absorb peak demand.
- Stored solar surplus can be used instead of curtailed.
- Every enrolled battery adds grid flexibility.
- Reposit trades the battery, supports grid needs, and creates customer value.

CTA language:

- Join the Grid Revolution.
- Join the fleet.
- Confirm - join the fleet.

Avoid:

- Unsupported carbon numbers.
- Generic "go green" language.
- Making impact feel separate from the actual offer.
- Overly technical market dispatch explanations before the customer understands their role.

## Journey Guidance

Awareness:

- Start with the grid problem: peak demand, gas peakers, solar curtailment.
- Position the customer's battery as underused climate infrastructure.
- Make joining the fleet feel concrete, not symbolic.

Consideration:

- Explain the three-part value chain: customer battery, Reposit coordination, cleaner/more flexible grid.
- Show savings and impact together.
- Use fleet proof and real mechanisms.

Conversion:

- Make Step 1 feel like joining the VPP/fleet.
- Reassure that usage data sizes the offer and fleet participation happens after confirmation.
- Keep the fixed fee visible so impact does not feel like a donation.

Onboarding:

- Welcome them to the fleet.
- Explain what their battery will do during peak and surplus events.
- Provide simple "impact moments" after dispatch events.

Retention:

- Send periodic fleet-impact updates.
- Connect household participation to broader grid outcomes.
- Keep financial benefit visible alongside impact.

## Objections And Responses

"Is this really greener?"

- Explain that coordinated batteries can reduce reliance on fossil backup during peak demand and store surplus renewable energy, with impact depending on grid conditions.

"Will I still save money?"

- The offer is based on the customer's usage profile and provides a fixed fee designed to improve their energy position.

"Is my one battery enough to matter?"

- One battery has limited impact alone; the fleet creates meaningful flexibility.

"Will my battery be used against my interests?"

- Explain customer plan terms, battery operating limits, and how value is shared.

"Are the impact claims measured?"

- Use only measured or supportable claims. Label indicative claims clearly.

## Content Guardrails

Do:

- Tie impact to grid mechanics.
- Use fleet language: join, coordinated, working together.
- Pair impact with savings.
- Keep disclaimers near impact claims.

Do not:

- Use vague sustainability copy as proof.
- Invent emissions numbers without source or model.
- Frame the customer as morally obligated.
- Hide the fixed-fee financial offer behind impact storytelling.

## Product/Journey Requirements

- Offer experience should include both money and impact language.
- Confirmation should feel like joining a fleet, not just buying a plan.
- Impact dashboards or emails should be considered part of retention.
- Manual bill fallback should maintain the impact frame: "we need this to size your offer and fleet role."
- Follow-up emails should use "your plan is ready - savings and impact, together."
