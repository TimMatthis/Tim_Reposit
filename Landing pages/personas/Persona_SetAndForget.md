# Persona 6: Set And Forget

> "I have better things to do than manage electricity."

The admin-averse buyer. Resents the energy industry not for being expensive, but for **demanding their attention**. The persona for whom Reposit's deepest promise — "you never think about electricity again" — is the entire product. Every other promise is decorative.

---

## 1. Snapshot

A homeowner aged **35–55**, often dual-income, time-poor, frequently with school-aged kids, often in tech / consulting / professional services. They installed solar and a battery on the recommendation of a brother-in-law or a neighbour, and they accepted that the *implied* deal was: "spend the capex now, and the energy problem goes away." It didn't. Instead they got an app, a dashboard, time-of-use rates, occasional retailer letters, and a vague nagging feeling that they should be *optimising something*. They are not anxious about the cost (that's Persona 5); they are **annoyed about the cognitive overhead**. They want energy to fall off their mental to-do list permanently.

---

## 2. Core driver

**Delegation.** Specifically: the elimination of energy as a category that requires their decisions, attention, or time. They will pay a small premium for total hands-off operation. They will pay nothing for "smart insights" that require them to do anything with those insights. The conversion lever is the literal phrase **"we handle everything."**

---

## 3. Inner monologue

- "I have not opened the energy app in four months and I refuse to feel guilty about it."
- "Why am I being asked to choose between five tariff structures? That's *your* job."
- "Just tell me what I'll pay and stop sending me emails about my dashboard."
- "I bought solar to *stop* thinking about electricity. The opposite happened."
- "If I have to compare retailers one more time I'm going to scream."
- "Send me one number. I'll send you that number every month. We're done."

---

## 4. Tone principles

1. **Terse.** Short sentences. Few clauses. Mirrors the brevity they crave.
2. **Concrete promises of removal.** Not "we make energy easier" — *"no apps, no dashboards, no comparisons, ever"*.
3. **Acknowledge the burnout, briefly.** A single line of empathy is enough — they don't want a long therapy session, they want the off-switch.
4. **Don't oversell intelligence.** Avoid "AI", "machine learning", "smart trading" — those phrases imply *they* will need to manage something. Frame the back-end as Reposit's job, full stop.
5. **Promise finality.** "The last time you'll ever think about this." This persona converts on endings, not beginnings.

---

## 5. Vocabulary

### Use
- "We handle everything"
- "Set and forget" *(only here — the literal phrase is on-driver)*
- "Zero decisions"
- "No apps to monitor"
- "No retailers to compare"
- "Just one fixed number"
- "Your time back"
- "The last time you'll ever think about this"
- "Boring on purpose"

### Avoid
- "Smart" *(implies more dashboards)*
- "Insights" / "analytics" / "data-driven" *(more reading)*
- "Customise your experience" *(the entire premise they're rejecting)*
- "Take control" *(off-driver — Persona 6 wants to surrender it, not seize it)*
- "Personalise" *(triggers "more decisions")*
- "Get the most out of your battery" *(implies optimisation work)*
- "Easy" *(too soft; "zero" is the right register)*

---

## 6. Hooks that work

1. **The dashboard-burnout hook.** *"You bought solar and a battery because someone said it would simplify your energy. Instead you got an app, a dashboard, time-of-use rates, and the nagging feeling you should be optimising something."* — names the exact frustration in the buyer's own words.
2. **The "boring is the product" hook.** *"Energy should be boring. Let's make it boring."* Reads as a permission slip.
3. **The zero-decisions hook.** *"Decisions required: zero."* — the outcome tile is the whole pitch.
4. **The retailer-fatigue hook.** *"No more retailer roulette."* Hits a specific, recurring source of the buyer's annoyance.
5. **The finality hook.** *"The last time you'll ever think about electricity."* Turns the click into a literal end-of-chapter moment.

---

## 7. Trust requirements

Before this persona will share their data, they need to believe:

- **Genuinely no app.** If a Reposit account or login is required for normal operation, the persona is gone. (Status checks should be possible if the buyer wants them, but not *required*.)
- **One number per month.** Same dollar amount, automatic, no decisions to make. They will read the validity panel to verify this.
- **Reposit handles retailer migration.** Switching electricity retailers is the single highest source of admin friction in their life. If Reposit takes that off their plate, that's the proof point.
- **Set-up takes minutes, not days.** The "60 seconds to connect, ~15 minutes to offer, we handle the rest" framing is more important here than for any other persona.
- **No future asks.** They will not engage if they suspect they'll be asked to do anything later — read a report, pick a new tariff, vote on a portfolio change. Promise of permanent silence is the close.

---

## 8. Anti-patterns

- **Dashboards in the marketing.** Even hero imagery showing an app screen kills the conversion. Don't show what they're trying to escape.
- **"Maximise your savings" framing.** The word "maximise" implies they have a job to do. Drop it.
- **Multiple tiers / packages.** This persona will not engage with three-package pickers. Show one number, not a comparison. (The three-door game we built for Persona 1 would be doubly off-brand here.)
- **Recurring-engagement hooks.** "Check in next month to see your savings" — the persona's whole point is they don't want to check in. Make any engagement strictly optional and quiet.
- **Personalisation theatre.** "Hi {first_name}, here's your tailored journey…" reads as more clutter. Be polite and minimal.

             because someone said it would simplify your energy.
             Instead you got an app, a dashboard, and the
             nagging feeling you should be optimising
             something."). Off-switch sentence ("Reposit takes
             over."). Finality sentence ("We trade your
             battery, replace your variable bill with one
             fixed fee, and you never think about electricity

The chaos pane is **the most important visual asset** for this persona. It must be visceral — phone screens, app notifications, mail from retailers — because the buyer is going to recognise their own home in it within 0.5 seconds. The calm pane should feel almost empty.

## 11. CTA verbs

The CTA should sound like the buyer handing the problem to someone else.

- ✅ **"Just Fix It For Me"** *(canonical — would be flippant for any other persona, but this is exactly the buyer's tone)*
- ✅ **"Take It Off My Plate"**
- ✅ **"Done. Send Me the Offer."**
- ❌ "Get Started" *(too procedural; implies more steps)*
- ❌ "Customise My Plan" *(off-driver — they want zero customisation)*
- ❌ "Take Control" *(opposite of what they're buying)*

The colloquial register *("Just Fix It For Me")* is on-brand for this persona only. Don't migrate it to other personas — it would read as flippant or unserious to retirees, analysts, or environmentalists.

| Chip | Reduces objection |
|---|---|
| **Truly set and forget** | "Will there be more admin later?" |
| **No more retailer roulette** | "What if I have to switch retailers again?" (You won't — Reposit does it.) |
| **Your time back** | "What's the actual outcome?" |
| **30-day satisfaction guarantee** *(if you have a fourth slot)* | "What if it isn't as hands-off as promised?" |

The first three are the persona's whole purchase rationale stated as three labels. Don't dilute them with "Read-only" or "No upfront fees" chips that suit other personas — those introduce concepts the buyer was happily not thinking about.

## 14. Conversion psychology summary

The Set And Forget buyer is not converting on intelligence, savings, or impact — they are converting on **the credible promise of mental quiet**. Your job in every artifact is to subtract: subtract decisions, subtract dashboards, subtract retailers, subtract follow-up emails, subtract everything that the energy industry has trained them to expect they'll have to manage. Every word that adds work for them — even good work — is a word that loses the click. Use the literal phrases this persona uses with themselves: "boring", "set and forget", "we handle everything", "zero decisions", "the last time you'll ever think about this". Lean into colloquial register on the CTA — *"Just Fix It For Me"* would feel unserious for a retiree or an analyst, but it is exactly how this buyer thinks at the moment of conversion. The whole funnel, from subject line to onboarding email, should feel like a quiet handover and then a closed door — and Reposit's job, from this moment on, is to make sure that door stays shut.

## 15. Email rules: pre-offer constraint

The **offer-ready email is sent before the offer is calculated** (immediately after the Solax/Sigenstor OAuth completes). At email-send time we do not yet know this user's annual saving, fixed monthly fee, or offer-expiry timestamp. The **only personalised element in the email body** is the deep link to *their* offer page (`{{ contact.retro_offer }}`).

For full token rules see [`README.md` → Email rule: nothing personalised except the URL](README.md#email-rule-nothing-personalised-except-the-url).

This persona is the **easiest one to keep generic**. They aren't reading the email for the number — they're reading it to confirm they don't have to do anything. The constraint is a near-perfect fit for their psychology.

### What this persona's email body must contain

| Element | Generic-true copy | Why it works for Set And Forget |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero tile** | *Decisions required* / **Zero** / *No tariff picking. No retailer switching. Ever. Your personalised offer is one tap away.* | The "Zero" hero is generic-true (every retrofit customer gets a zero-decision experience) and is the persona's whole purchase rationale stated as one number that *isn't* a dollar figure. |
| **Validity** | *"Locked for **24 hours** from when your offer is generated. Open it when you have a minute. We'll handle the install, the paperwork, and the retailer swap."* | Reframes the 24-hour hold as a "no rush" message rather than urgency. "We'll handle..." is the literal subtraction-of-tasks this persona is buying. |
| **Closing finality line** | *"One minute of your time. Then we handle everything. Forever."* | Already on the landing page. Belongs in the email too as the final beat before the CTA repeat. |
| **Footer date** | *"...because you recently connected your battery to Reposit."* | No specific timestamp. No first-name greeting. |
| **CTA** | *"Just Fix It For Me →"* on a single green button → `{{ contact.retro_offer }}`. | Colloquial. The persona's own internal voice. The constraint actually *helps* — without a personalised number, there's no risk that the CTA pivots from "fix it" to "review it". |

### Email anti-patterns specific to Set And Forget

- **No numbers, charts, comparisons, or calculations anywhere in the email body.** This persona did not open the email to do maths. The hero tile may say "Zero" (a label, not a calculation); it may not say "$X saved per year".
- **No "review your offer" / "verify the assumptions" / "check the working" framing.** That's Battery ROI's email, not this one. Their click motivation is "tap and stop thinking", not "tap and verify".
- **No mention of an app, a dashboard, or an "insights" portal.** Even teasing one in the email body undermines the entire promise. They want a closed door, not a new tab.
- **No multi-paragraph hero subcopy.** Maximum two short sentences. They are reading on the couch, on the way somewhere, while the kettle boils. Anything denser loses them.
- **No "Limited time"-style countdowns.** They open the email when they get to it. The validity panel must say *"open it when you have a minute"* — and mean it.
- **No follow-up-tease language** ("we'll send you a reminder if you haven't tapped"). Implying a *next* email subtracts mental quiet, which is exactly what they're buying.
- **No greeting by first name.** Anonymous-safe is non-negotiable for this persona too — first-name greetings imply ongoing relationship-management, which contradicts the closed-door promise.

### Tease-the-click lines that work for this persona

These are the only acceptable ways to refer to the personalised numbers *without* showing them:

- "Your personalised offer is one tap away."
- "Open it when you have a minute. Nothing to read in the meantime."
- "One tap. Then we handle everything. Forever."
- "We've done the maths. Tap to confirm."

The actual calculated figures live on the offer page the user clicks through to — and even there, this persona will skim past them straight to the green button. **The email is the door, not the room. For this persona, the door should also be very, very quiet.**
