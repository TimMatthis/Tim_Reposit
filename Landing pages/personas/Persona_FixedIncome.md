# Persona 2: Fixed Income

> "My income is fixed. My energy bill should be too."

The retiree / pensioner segment. The most emotionally exposed of the six personas, and the one where a single careless word can lose the entire campaign. Treat this guide as a guardrail, not a starting point.

---

## 1. Snapshot

A homeowner aged **62–80**, often a couple, on a fixed income — most commonly the Age Pension, sometimes a defined-benefit super pension or a steady annuity drawdown. They live in their own home, often paid off, and they bought solar + battery 2–8 years ago precisely because they were trying to *take a future variable cost off their balance sheet*. The system did not fully deliver on that promise. They notice every quarterly bill increase because their income does not move to compensate. They make purchase decisions slowly, talk them over with their partner and often an adult child, and rely heavily on the phone and email rather than chat.

---

## 2. Core driver

**Budget certainty.** Specifically: the absence of nasty surprises in the line items their fortnight is built around. Money is not the deepest issue — *unpredictability* is. They will pay a slightly higher fixed fee than a slightly lower variable one, every time, if it removes the quarterly knot in their stomach.

---

## 3. Inner monologue

- "We had a $740 bill in winter. We only just covered it."
- "I'm not going to start fiddling with apps and tariffs at my age."
- "If we get hit with another spike like that we'll have to skip the heater."
- "I want to know what it costs every month, full stop."
- "Can I actually call someone if something goes wrong?"
- "Helen down the road got tricked into switching retailers and her bill went up. I'm not doing that."

---

## 4. Tone principles

1. **Empathy first.** Acknowledge the lived experience before offering the product. "You planned for retirement" lands harder than any feature list.
2. **Slower pace.** Shorter sentences, more white space, larger type sizes (16 px body minimum). Don't rush the reader.
3. **Plain language only.** No "wholesale market", no "VPP", no "kWh" except when illustrating a comparison. "Trades your battery while you sleep" is fine. "Optimises spot-price arbitrage" is not.
4. **Human reassurance.** Mention real people, real phone numbers, real Australians. This persona is hyper-aware of being routed to a chatbot.
5. **Frame money in groceries, not percentages.** "$346/year is roughly four weeks of groceries" beats "12% saving" every time.

---

## 5. Vocabulary

### Use
- "Same amount, every month"
- "Same in summer. Same in winter."
- "Fits the budget"
- "Real people you can call"
- "No surprises on the fortnight"
- "We handle the paperwork"
- "Already trusted by hundreds of retirees" *(only if true; see Trust requirements)*
- "Cents per day, predictable"

### Avoid
- "Wholesale market" / "spot price" / "arbitrage" / "VPP"
- "Trade your battery" *(reads as risky; rephrase as "puts your battery to work")*
- "Cutting-edge", "world-first", "innovative" — these read as untested
- "Disrupt" / "revolution" / "movement" *(this persona dislikes disruption by definition)*
- "Click here" / "tap to reveal" *(reads as scammy)*
- Any reference to crypto, blockchain, or AI — even if technically accurate

---

## 6. Hooks that work

1. **"Fixed income, fixed bill" parallel.** The strongest single line for this persona is the literal symmetry: *"A fixed income deserves a fixed energy bill."*
2. **Winter spike memory.** Every retiree remembers their largest bill of the last two years. Anchor against it: "No more $740 winters."
3. **Groceries-equivalent framing.** Translate the annual saving into something tangible — *"$346 a year is roughly four weeks of groceries back."*
4. **"Helen down the road" social proof.** "Trusted by 2,000+ Australian households — including many retirees" closes the trust gap that "household" alone can't.
5. **"You don't need to do anything"** delegation hook. Critically, this is *not* "set and forget" tech-speak — it's "we handle the install, paperwork, and retailer for you".

---

## 7. Trust requirements

Before this persona will even share their data, they need to believe:

- They will speak to **a real Australian person** if something goes wrong, on a normal landline-friendly number, during normal hours.
- The **fixed monthly fee will not change** without their consent. No "subject to market" footnotes.
- Their **existing equipment is safe**. No risk of damage, no warranty voiding, no overnight changes to how their solar/battery behaves.
- There is **no payment today and no penalty for walking away**. They will read this twice.
- The figure shown is **realistic for someone like them**, not the most aggressive case study.

---

## 8. Anti-patterns

- **Game mechanics of any kind.** No reveals, no doors, no blurs. They will close the email.
- **Faux urgency.** "Only 3 spots left!" is read instantly as a scam. Use the genuine 24-hour offer hold instead, framed gently.
- **Tech triumphalism.** "AI-powered trading platform" loses this persona. "We do it for you" wins them.
- **Personalisation that's too clever.** "Hi Margaret, I noticed you've been thinking about energy lately…" reads as creepy surveillance. Stick to first name only, or no name at all.
- **Multiple tones in one piece.** Don't mix activist clarion calls (Persona 4) with this voice. They cancel each other out.

## 10. Hero formula

```
PANE A     : What retirement shouldn't feel like   (chaos pane)
PANE B     : You, with Reposit                     (calm pane)
H1         : A fixed income deserves
             a fixed energy bill.
SUB        : Empathy sentence ("You planned for retirement.").
             Mechanism sentence in plain English ("Reposit
             connects your battery to the market and replaces
             the variable bill with one fixed monthly fee").
             Promise sentence ("Same amount every month.").
TAGLINE    : "You and Reposit: Better together"
```

The chaos/calm split is *more important* for this persona than for any other — it lets the buyer see their current life ("That's me, that's exactly what winter feels like") before the alternative is offered.

---

## 11. CTA verbs

This buyer will not click an aggressive verb. The CTA should feel like a request for information, not a transaction.

- ✅ **"Lock In My Fixed Bill"** *(canonical)*
- ✅ **"See What I'd Pay Each Month"**
- ✅ **"Show Me My Monthly Fee"**
- ❌ "Get Started" *(implies they're committing)*
- ❌ "Buy Now" / "Subscribe" *(commerce grammar)*
- ❌ "Activate My Plan" *(too active)*

The CTA's verb is the buyer's own intention, written in first person. The colour and shape should not change from the master button — green gradient (`#2de49d → #15b876`), 11 px radius, but consider 17–18 px body type inside the button (one tick larger than other personas) to suit older eyes.

## 12. Trust chips (3–4)

| Chip | Reduces objection |
|---|---|
| **Same amount, every month** | "Will the bill spike?" |
| **No complicated technology** | "Will I have to learn an app?" |
| **Real people you can call** | "Who do I talk to if something breaks?" |
| **30-day satisfaction guarantee** | "What if I change my mind?" |

If you only have room for three, drop *No complicated technology* — but only because the absence-of-tech is implied by the calm hero. Never drop *Real people you can call*.

---

## 14. Conversion psychology summary

The Fixed Income buyer is not converting on a feature — they are converting on the **promise that next quarter looks identical to this one**. Your job in every artifact is to remove unpredictability, not to add value. Acknowledge the lived experience first ("You planned for retirement"), then offer the simplest possible mechanism for restoring predictability, then anchor it in concrete dollars-and-groceries language so the proof is felt, not parsed. Avoid every tactic — game mechanics, urgency theatre, tribe rhetoric, tech vocabulary — that creates even momentary anxiety; this buyer's nervous system is the conversion bottleneck and you are either soothing it or losing them. When in doubt, use shorter sentences, larger type, and the literal phrase "same amount, every month."

## 15. Email rules: pre-offer constraint

The **offer-ready email is sent before the offer is calculated** (immediately after the Solax/Sigenstor OAuth completes). At email-send time we do not yet know this user's annual saving, fixed monthly fee, or offer-expiry timestamp. The **only personalised element in the email body** is the deep link to *their* offer page (`{{ contact.retro_offer }}`).

For full token rules see [`README.md` → Email rule: nothing personalised except the URL](README.md#email-rule-nothing-personalised-except-the-url).

### What this persona's email body must contain

| Element | Generic-true copy | Why it works for Fixed Income |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero tile** | *What you'd pay* / **One fixed fee** / *Same in summer. Same in winter. Your personalised number is inside.* | The structural promise (a fee, not a bill) is what this buyer signed up to read about. The exact figure can wait until the offer page. |
| **Validity** | *"Your monthly fee is locked for **24 hours** from when your offer is generated. No payment today. Walk away any time before you confirm."* | Calm logistic reality + explicit reassurance about commitment. Three anxieties answered in one panel. |
| **Footer date** | *"...because you recently connected your battery to Reposit."* | No specific timestamp. No first-name greeting. Body type stays at 16 px so retired eyes can read it on a phone. |
| **CTA** | *"Lock In My Fixed Bill →"* on a single green button → `{{ contact.retro_offer }}`. | "Lock in" is the verb this persona thinks in. |
| **Support reassurance** | *"Prefer to talk first? Call us on 1800 773 851."* + *"Real people you can call"* trust chip. | This support promise is conversion-critical for this persona, but only if the number is real and current. Never use placeholder phone details. |

### Email anti-patterns specific to Fixed Income

- **No placeholder dollar figures with disclaimers.** "From $X/month*" or "as low as $X" — even with footnoted small print — reads as the exact retailer language that landed them on a bill they can't predict. **Prefer "no number" over "hedged number"** in the email body.
- **No "indicative" or "estimated" weasel words** anywhere in the body copy. They corrode the entire promise of fixedness. Save the disclaimer for the offer page where it belongs.
- **No countdowns or expiry timers.** This buyer panics under deadline pressure. The 24-hour hold is mentioned once, gently, framed as "no payment today, walk away any time".
- **No "exciting" language.** This buyer wants boring. "Your offer is ready" beats "Your offer has arrived!" by a measurable margin.
- **No greeting by first name.** Reposit is anonymous-safe; pretending otherwise breaks trust faster than any other tactic for this persona.

### Tease-the-click lines that work for this persona

These are the only acceptable ways to refer to the personalised numbers *without* showing them:

- "Your personalised number is inside."
- "Your number is calculated from your actual usage. No payment today."
- "We'll show you the monthly figure when you tap through. Cancel any time."
- "Your fixed monthly fee is one tap away."

The actual calculated figures live on the offer page the user clicks through to. **The email is the door, not the room.**
