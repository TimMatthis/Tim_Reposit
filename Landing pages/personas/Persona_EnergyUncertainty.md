# Persona 5: Energy Uncertainty

> "I can't control the market. I can control my bill."

The anxiety-led buyer. Not financially fragile — emotionally fatigued. Has been hit by enough surprise quarterly bills, news headlines, and tariff changes that the *unpredictability itself* has become the problem. The persona for whom **"boring is beautiful"** is not a tagline but a literal product attribute.

---

## 1. Snapshot

A homeowner aged **35–60**, dual-income or self-employed, often with a mortgage, often with kids. Reads the news and notices the energy stories. Has had at least one bill in the last 18 months that materially exceeded what they budgeted for — and the memory of it sits between their shoulder blades whenever a new statement arrives. They are *not* in financial distress; they can absorb a $400 surprise. But they hate the *cognitive cost* of bracing for it. They installed solar and a battery hoping it would lower the variance. It didn't, quite. They're now looking for the missing piece that finally lets them stop checking their app every Sunday night.

---

## 2. Core driver

**The relief of certainty.** Specifically: the cessation of low-grade, ambient anxiety about a thing they cannot influence. They do not need the lowest possible bill — they need a **bill they can predict at the start of the month** and then forget about. Variance is the enemy. Even a *worse* fixed price beats a *better* variable one for this persona, within reason.

---

## 3. Inner monologue

- "Every time I see 'wholesale prices spike' in the headlines I think *here we go again*."
- "I budget for a winter bill of $800. It came in at $1,200. That's the third time."
- "I don't even know what my time-of-use rates are anymore. They keep changing."
- "I just want to know what it costs and stop having to think about it."
- "If something goes wrong with the wholesale market, I want it to be Reposit's problem, not mine."
- "I bought solar to escape this. Why am I still anxious about Sunday-night bills?"

---

## 4. Tone principles

1. **Calm, almost flat.** Match the relief you're promising. No exclamation marks, no caps, no "amazing news!".
2. **Name the volatility specifically.** "Tariffs, fuel prices, wholesale markets, the weather" beats "things you can't control".
3. **Externalise the threat.** Frame the cause as **out there** (markets, retailers, weather), and the solution as a **boundary** the buyer puts between themselves and it.
4. **Sell stillness.** The conversion promise is "you stop thinking about this". Lean into that — even at the cost of seeming under-energetic.
5. **Honesty about what stays the same.** Be explicit that nothing changes about their existing setup, their hardware, their property. The peace of mind is in *what they no longer have to track*, not in a new system to learn.

---

## 5. Vocabulary

### Use
- "One fixed monthly fee"
- "Below your current average"
- "No more bill-spike anxiety"
- "You can't control the market"
- "Boring is beautiful"
- "Stop checking your bill"
- "Predictable, every month"
- "Locked in"
- "Held for 24 hours" *(genuine offer-validity language)*

### Avoid
- "Volatile" / "rollercoaster" / "doom" — amplifies the anxiety you're trying to soothe
- "Beat the system" *(you're not selling a hack — you're selling an exit)*
- "Don't worry about a thing" *(reads as condescension to anxious adults)*
- "Take control" *(they don't want control — they want to be off-duty)*
- "Smart" *(reads as more dashboards to learn)*

---

## 6. Hooks that work

1. **The control-asymmetry hook.** *"You can't control the market. You can control your bill."* — names the impossible thing and the possible thing in one sentence. Highest-converting line for this persona.
2. **The Sunday-night hook.** *"No more refreshing the bill app on a Sunday night."* Specific, lived, and tied to a real ritual the buyer wants to end.
3. **The bill-spike memory hook.** *"Fine for months — then the bill you didn't budget for."* Acknowledges the pattern they've already noticed.
4. **The "boring is beautiful" hook.** *"Energy should be boring. Let's make it boring."* — gives them permission to want what they actually want.
5. **The "below your current average" hook.** Turns the fixed price from a constraint into a quiet upgrade. Specifically *below*, not *roughly equal*.

---

## 7. Trust requirements

Before this persona will share their data, they need to believe:

- The **fixed fee is genuinely fixed** for the term — no "subject to wholesale", no fine-print escalation clauses.
- If wholesale markets melt down, **Reposit absorbs the volatility**, not the customer. They need to see this stated, not implied.
- There is a **30-day satisfaction guarantee** or equivalent — a clean exit if the relief doesn't materialise.
- **No payment is taken today**. The persona is anxious about commitment as well as bills; both must be defused.
- The **24-hour offer hold** is real, not a sales tactic. The reason it exists (data freshness) should be stated.

---

## 8. Anti-patterns

- **Theatrical urgency.** Countdown timers, "only 12 hours left!" — this persona reads them as the very volatility they're trying to escape, and the email gets deleted.
- **Game mechanics.** Blurred numbers, "tap to reveal", three-door reveals — they all elevate cortisol and lose the conversion. **This is the persona where the spammy email risk hits hardest.**
- **Activist clarion calls.** "Join the smart energy revolution" reads as more noise. They want quiet.
- **Comparing yourself to "other retailers".** Even if you're better, the comparison reactivates the anxiety they're trying to leave behind.
- **Big numbers in the hero.** Lead with the *kind* of saving, not the dollar figure — the dollar figure should appear after the buyer has already calmed down.

---

## 9. Subject + preheader patterns

| Subject | Preheader | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| "A fixed price for your electricity" | "Same number every month. Below your current average. Held for 24 hours." | Boring on purpose. Beautiful by consequence. |
| "Stop bracing for the next bill" | "Your retrofit offer is a fixed monthly fee — predictable, every month." | Names the ritual the buyer wants to end. |
| "Your retrofit offer (and the end of bill anxiety)" | "One fixed fee. Below what you pay today. No payment today." | The parenthetical does the persuasion; the body does the reassurance. |

Avoid: dollar figures in the subject line, "$0", or anything that reads like a deal-of-the-week. They've trained themselves to skip it.

## 10. Hero formula

```
PANE A     : Everyone else right now   (chaos pane — the bill
             rollercoaster, headlines about wholesale prices,
             retailer letters with rate increases)
PANE B     : You, with Reposit   (calm pane — a single flat
             line at the fixed fee, month after month)
H1         : You can't control the market.
             You can control your bill.
SUB        : Threat sentence ("Tariffs, fuel prices, wholesale
             markets, the weather — none of it asks your
             permission"). Boundary sentence ("Your battery is
             already on the wall. It just needs a promotion.").
             Outcome sentence ("Reposit replaces your variable
             bill with one fixed monthly fee — set below what
             you pay today.").
TAGLINE    : "You and Reposit: Better together"
```

The chaos pane shows what *everyone else* is dealing with — not the buyer's own life. The framing is *"you've been smart enough to install solar; you're already most of the way out of this; here's the last move"*.

## 11. CTA verbs

The CTA should sound like the buyer's own quiet decision, not an action.

- ✅ **"Get My Fixed Price"** *(canonical)*
- ✅ **"See What I'd Pay"**
- ✅ **"Lock In My Number"**
- ❌ "Take Control" *(off-driver — they want to be off-duty)*
- ❌ "Get Started" *(too active)*
- ❌ "Activate My Plan" *(too procedural; reads as more admin)*

The button colour stays Reposit green; the language stays in the indicative ("see", "get", "lock") rather than the imperative ("take", "claim", "activate").

## 14. Conversion psychology summary

The Energy Uncertainty buyer is not converting on a feature — they are converting on **the feeling of putting the bill out of their head**. Your job in every artifact is to lower the room temperature. Calm voice, flat affect, plain language, no hype, no game, no urgency theatre. The promise is not "you'll save money" (though they will) — it is **"you'll stop thinking about this"**. Every word in the artifact should be auditioning for that promise: if it raises cortisol, it's wrong. The control-asymmetry hook (*"You can't control the market. You can control your bill."*) is the single highest-converting line because it does the persona's emotional work in one sentence — it acknowledges what's outside their power, and offers the precise, narrow thing that's inside it. The CTA is the buyer's own internal sigh of relief, written in a button: *get my fixed price*.

## 15. Email rules: pre-offer constraint

The **offer-ready email is sent before the offer is calculated** (immediately after the Solax/Sigenstor OAuth completes). At email-send time we do not yet know this user's annual saving, fixed monthly fee, or offer-expiry timestamp. The **only personalised element in the email body** is the deep link to *their* offer page (`{{ contact.retro_offer }}`).

For full token rules see [`README.md` → Email rule: nothing personalised except the URL](README.md#email-rule-nothing-personalised-except-the-url).

This persona is buying **certainty**. That makes the constraint a feature, not a bug: an email body with no wobbling figures actually models the steadiness this buyer is paying for.

### What this persona's email body must contain

| Element | Generic-true copy | Why it works for Energy Uncertainty |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero tile** | *What you'd pay* / **One fixed fee** / *Same. Every. Month. Below your current average. Your personalised number is inside.* | "Same. Every. Month." is the literal phrase this buyer's nervous system is searching for. Three full stops, three commitments. |
| **Validity** | *"Your fixed monthly fee is locked for **24 hours** from when your offer is generated. No payment today. 30-day satisfaction guarantee. Nothing changes on your existing setup."* | Three reassurances stacked together — the exact wizard-reassurance pattern that already works on the landing page. The 30-day guarantee is **non-negotiable** in this email. |
| **Footer date** | *"...because you recently connected your battery to Reposit."* | Generic, low-temperature. No first-name greeting (would raise the temperature). |
| **CTA** | *"Get My Fixed Price →"* on a single green button → `{{ contact.retro_offer }}`. | The buyer's internal sigh of relief. Singular intent. |

### Email anti-patterns specific to Energy Uncertainty

- **No "indicative", "estimated", "subject to change", or "approximate"** anywhere in the body. Every weasel word is a small wobble in the promise of certainty. This persona reads them as **structural** uncertainty, not legal disclaimer. Save that language for the offer page footnotes.
- **No countdown timers, expiry banners, or urgency theatre.** "Limited time only" raises cortisol and triggers the anxiety response that loses the click. State the 24-hour hold once, in the validity panel, framed as logistic reality.
- **No placeholder dollar figures even with disclaimers.** This persona will *prefer* an email with no number than an email with a hedged number. Hedging is the cortisol trigger.
- **No comparison tables in the email body.** Save the variable-vs-fixed comparison for the offer page where the methodology is visible. The email should feel quieter than the landing page, not louder.
- **No "your offer has been unlocked!" / "exciting news!" / exclamation marks anywhere.** Flat affect throughout. "Your offer is ready" is the highest pitch this email allows.
- **No "join" / "unlock" / "transform" / "revolution" / movement language.** Wrong nervous system.

### Tease-the-click lines that work for this persona

These are the only acceptable ways to refer to the personalised numbers *without* showing them:

- "Your personalised number is inside."
- "Your fixed monthly figure is one tap away."
- "Same number every month. Confirmed when you tap through."
- "No payment today. 30-day guarantee. Walk away any time before you confirm."

The actual calculated figures live on the offer page the user clicks through to. **The email is the door, not the room** — and for this persona specifically, the email's job is to be a *quiet* door. If a writer can't decide whether a line is too loud, cut it.
